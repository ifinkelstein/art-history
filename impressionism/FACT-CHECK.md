# Fact-check report

Every factual claim in the lesson was checked against museum pages, Wikimedia, and
art-history sources. Summary: **no claims were found to be false.** A handful are
popular shorthand or have small source-to-source variation; those are flagged
below and were softened in the text where it mattered. Links were all opened and
confirmed live (not generated from memory).

## Confirmed as stated

- **Debussy (1862–1918), "first Impressionist composer."** Widely described this
  way; the label was borrowed from the painters. Source: USUO/Utah Symphony,
  "Building a Collection: Clair de Lune."
- **Debussy answered "Mon plaisir" ("My pleasure")** when asked what rule he
  followed. Source: The Conversation, "Decoding Debussy's Clair de Lune."
- **La Mer (1905) used Hokusai's Great Wave on its cover.** Source: The Conversation (same).
- **Salon des Refusés, 1863; Cabanel's Birth of Venus bought by Napoleon III.**
  Sources: Wikipedia (Cabanel), Musée d'Orsay, Obelisk/Art History Project.
- **Zola's "pink and white marzipan" jab** at Cabanel's smooth style. Source:
  Musée d'Orsay artwork page; Google Arts & Culture.
- **Cézanne on Monet: "only an eye — but my God, what an eye!"** Source:
  Artsper Magazine; widely quoted.
- **"Impressionist" began as an insult** from critic/humorist Louis Leroy
  reviewing the 1874 show. Source: Google Arts & Culture, "6 Things About Monet."
- **Seurat died at 31 (1859–1891); hid his partner Madeleine and their baby; La
  Grande Jatte took ~2 years, 70+ studies, ~10 ft wide; critics cried "bedlam,
  scandal, hilarity."** Sources: Art Institute of Chicago (Seurat page &
  exhibition), Britannica, Wikipedia.
- **Monet Haystacks: the proper series is 25 canvases, 1890–91; stacks ~15–20 ft,
  just outside his Giverny farmhouse; he worked several canvases at once; "the sun
  sets so fast I cannot follow it"; 15 hung together at Durand-Ruel, May 1891; up
  close the stack is browns/reds/purples.** Sources: Art Institute of Chicago
  artwork pages (1985.1103, 1933.1155, 1983.166), Wikipedia, TheCollector,
  thehistoryofart.org.
- **A late haystack dazzled Kandinsky in 1896.** Source: TheCollector.
- **Pigment timeline & paint tube.** Cadmium yellow ~1820, viridian 1838,
  cerulean blue 1860; the 1800s introduced more pigments than the prior two
  millennia; the collapsible metal paint tube was patented in 1841 (by the
  American painter John Goffe Rand); natural ultramarine was famously costlier
  than gold (ground lapis lazuli); Pissarro said he banished "earth" colors; Van
  Gogh used the cheap new chrome yellow. Sources: WebExhibits "Pigments through
  the Ages: Industrialization," eclecticlight.co, Jackson's Art, inbedwithmonalisa.

## Flagged nuances (popular shorthand — softened in the text)

1. **"Van Gogh sold only one painting in his life."** This is the traditional
   story (The Red Vineyard, 1890). Modern scholarship notes he sold/traded a few
   other works and drawings, so "only one" is the popular shorthand. The HTML now
   says **"barely sold a single painting"** to stay safe.

2. **Cobalt blue year.** Sources give ~1802 (first synthesis) to ~1807
   (commercial). The HTML now says **"~1802–07."**

3. **The Renoir paint-tube quote.** "Without paint in tubes there would have been
   no Impressionism" is famously attributed to Renoir (via his son Jean Renoir's
   memoir). The HTML now says Renoir **"is often quoted as saying."**

4. **Degas "1,500+ ballet works."** An approximation (dancers are roughly half of
   his ~2,000 works). The HTML now says **"roughly 1,500."**

5. **Monet "25 times."** The catalogued *Haystacks/Stacks of Wheat* series proper
   is 25 canvases (Wildenstein 1266–1290); he painted a few earlier stack pictures
   too, so total stack paintings run to ~30. "25" (the series) is correct as used.

6. **Monet "tried to drown himself" / "couldn't afford paint" / Camille deathbed
   guilt.** All documented biographical episodes (the Seine episode ~1868 from his
   letters; the deathbed-colour remark from Monet's later recollection to
   Clemenceau). Kept as engaging-but-true.
